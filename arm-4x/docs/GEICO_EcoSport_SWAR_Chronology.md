## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (Strongsuit AI Generated)

George Da Costa, a disabled individual, was involved in a motor vehicle collision caused by GEICO's insured. His attorney-in-fact, Becca Travis, formally requested ADA accommodations for written communication and provided explicit instructions regarding vehicle repairs and payments. GEICO, through various representatives, repeatedly disregarded these instructions, misrepresented facts, and made multiple unauthorized payments to Woody Anderson Ford (WAF). GEICO then unilaterally terminated Da Costa's rental car benefits under false pretenses, later admitting the pretext was invalid. WAF, under manager Brian Bronson, released the vehicle in an unsafe and inoperable condition after Travis signed a release under duress, with a Ford Senior Engineer subsequently condemning the repairs. These facts suggest potential violations of the ADA, including retaliation, and various Alabama torts such as negligent undertaking, suppression, misrepresentation, and wantonness.

## ALABAMA LEGAL FRAMEWORK - POTENTIAL CLAIMS

### 1. NEGLIGENT UNDERTAKING
GEICO assumed duty by agreeing to pay for repairs and rental on behalf of their insured. Evidence shows improper handling of third-party claim using first-party restrictions for 44 days before admitting error.

### 2. WANTONNESS VS. NEGLIGENCE  
GEICO's continued improper handling after being informed of correct claim classification may bypass Alabama's contributory negligence defense and allow punitive damages.

### 3. UNLAWFUL RETALIATION (ADA)
Rental termination occurred within 72 hours of ADA accommodation request - pattern suggests retaliatory conduct.

### 4. FRAUDULENT SUPPRESSION
GEICO took 44 days to confirm what the police report established on DAY ONE - that this was a third-party claim. During those 44 days, they applied first-party restrictions they knew were improper.
